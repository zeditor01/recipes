Worked Examples for Mainframe Data Integration Patterns.

Mainframe data is the ESSENTIAL RAW MATERIAL for the following critical initiatives in Enterprises (that use mainframes).

  1. Continuous Transaction Processing to support the Core Business
  2. Develop and Serve AI models to help automate and govern those core business transactions
  3. Deploy realtime Data APIs to serve the needs of Cloud-based systems of engagement with clients

The architectural challenge is to find the best way(s) to create APIs ( for transactions, data access, and AI scoring ) based on the mainframe data, to support these initiatives.

"Zeditor" is a practical cookbook for the 3 major patterns used for accessing and exploiting data that is created and managed on IBM Mainframe Systems. It comprises documented implementation audit trails, demonstration videos, and access to hands-on test drive systems

These patterns will cause fierce debate between advocates of competing approaches. Some points of view will be objective, and some will be subjective or even dogmatic. In reality, a mixture of the patterns is likely to be required by most enterprises, because different use cases have different needs for consuming mainframe data. "Zeditor" attempts to bypass the (sometimes dogmatic) views presented by each camp, and focus on practical worked examples of how to implement each pattern.

The Right-Shift pattern is undoubtedly the most widely adopted pattern in Enterprises at the moment. It is also the pattern with the most technical debt and complexity. Heterogeneous Data Replication are challenging, largely because of the project management challenges of the number of different technical departments (insourced and outsourced) that all need to work together. The diagram below is perhaps a more realistic representation of the relative difficulty of making the Right-Shift pattern work.

The author is NOT seeking to trash the Right-Shift pattern. The author is frustrated that many organisations opt for the Right-Shift pattern without fully evaluating the relative merits of the other patterns. It is a fairly common occurance that funding is available for a specific business project only, and the benefits of a broader architecture are simply "outside scope". The goal of these "Zeditor" resources is to make the reader aware of all three patterns,so that an informed decision can be taken.

Meet the (Z)editor

Neale Armstrong works for IBM Australia as a System Z Data Management Technical Specialist. Born a Pom, I had the opportunity to transfer "down under" and live in Australia. Despite being an Aussie citizen now, I confess to being conflicted during Ashes series. On weekends and holidays I can transform into a "mamil" or a "Grey Nomad" as I explore parts of this wonderful country.

My motivation for creating this website was to become more efficient at my job by creating re-useable content for the client projects that I get involved with. For example, when advising multiple clients about heterogeeous CDC deployments I found that 80% of the discussion content was common to all clients, so decided to document worked examples that were genericly indicative for all use cases. It was helpful to be able to refer clients to worked examples for the majority of their questions, and then address client-specific questions directly.

Furthermore, by providing side-by-side worked examples of all three major patterns for mainframe data integration, I am able to present alternative architectural patterns that may be helpful to a Client's scenario.

I hope that these worked examples are of practical help to you as a reader.
